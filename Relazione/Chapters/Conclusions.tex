\chapter*{Conclusions}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Conclusions}

This project, using the Shelly Plug S (Gen~1) as a concrete testing ground, analyzes the balance that is created between cost, usability, and security in the modern Internet of Things (IoT) landscape. By adopting both offensive and defensive perspectives.

\medskip
\noindent The project development began with the physical analysis of the device and the extraction of its binary firmware via the exposed UART interface. This initial phase highlighted a common paradigm in legacy consumer IoT, that of relying on plaintext protocols and unprotected storage to minimize development overhead and unit costs. The discovery of critical network credentials, cloud encryption keys, and device identifiers stored in plaintext within the flash memory, combined with the use of unencrypted MQTT and HTTP protocols, confirmed that the device lacked transport-layer and storage-level protection. 

\medskip
\noindent In the second phase, we translated these findings into a practical demonstration of risk by developing a custom firmware capable of silently exfiltrating telemetry data via UDP while maintaining behavioral camouflage via MQTT to prevent detection. The successful deployment of this firmware through network-level hijacking (ARP poisoning and DNS spoofing) and the direct abuse of the unauthenticated OTA endpoint demonstrated how easily an attacker within the local network can compromise device integrity. Crucially, the subsequent analysis of the exfiltrated power consumption profiles showed that the privacy risks associated with compromised smart plugs extend far beyond simple control interception, enabling the reconstruction of user habits.

\medskip
\noindent In the final phase of the project, robust mitigation strategies were implemented directly on the target hardware. Despite the severe memory constraints of the ESP8266 SoC, we successfully migrated the system to MQTTS with mutual TLS (mTLS) authentication using ECC certificates, ensuring both transport confidentiality and strong bidirectional identity verification. Simultaneously, the implementation of an RSA-2048 signature verification scheme utilizing PKCS\#1 v1.5 padding and SHA-256 hashing successfully secured the OTA update interface, preventing any future execution of unauthorized code. Finally, the analysis with Wireshark validated the effectiveness of these interventions, showing that all previously cleartext telemetry data was rendered entirely illegible to unauthorized observers.

\medskip
\noindent In conclusion, this project demonstrates that security cannot be treated as a secondary feature in the consumer IoT landscape. The vulnerabilities analyzed in this legacy Shelly Plug S (Gen 1) are representative of an era where hardware constraints were frequently cited to justify the omission of cryptography, authentication and other security standards. However, as the IoT landscape transitions to more modern architectures (such as the ESP32-based Gen~2 and Gen~3 lines), adopting a "Security-by-Design" paradigm is no longer just a best practice, but a fundamental requirement to protect user privacy and ensure confidentiality, integrity and authenticity.
